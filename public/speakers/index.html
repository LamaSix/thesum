Meet the Speakers

Candy Chang

  • Senior TED Fellow
  • Creator of Before I Die
  • website
  • twitter
  • facebook
  • instagram
“Creativity thrives in a comfortable place where you can be vulnerable and wander in an uninhibited world of what ifs. Good ideas come from staying curious, trying things out, and allowing random experiences to become meaningful to you.”
Candy Chang is an artist, designer, and urban planner who makes cities more comfortable for people. A Senior TED Fellow, Chang is passionate about redefining the ways we share information in public space to improve our neighborhoods. The Atlantic calls her recent work, Before I Die, “one of the most creative community projects ever.”
More here.

Zach Klein

  • Co-founder and CEO of DIY.org
  • Co-founded and designed Vimeo
  • website
  • twitter
“For any community to be creative there must be cheap access to simple tools capable of complex expression plus a culture of fearlessness.”
Zach Klein is co-founder and CEO of DIY, a platform for kids to learn any skill by just trying. He’s also co-founded and once designed Vimeo. He’s a partner at Founder Collective, the seed-stage venture capital firm based in New York City, and formerly a faculty member at the School of Visual Arts’ MFA program in Interaction Design. He’s a passionate builder of offline and online communities, self-reliance, and creative literacy.

Billy Sorrentino

  • Creative Director at WIRED
  • instagram
  • twitter
“People argue all the time about what ‘design’ means. Design is how we shape experiences for the better. Don’t be afraid to fail. I fuck up constantly, and in the end it always makes me more creative.”
Billy Sorrentino is the Creative Director for WIRED, where he drives the brand’s innovative approach to design, ensuring it’s at the forefront of everything WIRED creates. Sorrentino leads the brand’s four creative departments: design, photo, video and production, and is responsible for cross-channel brand presence, including print, desktop, digital editions, mobile, social media and live events.

Scott Doorley

  • Creative Director at the Standford d.school
  • Coauthor of Make Space
  • website
  • twitter
“Some Rules for Creative Work:
Relentlessly search for inspiration.
Make things.
Name them.
Share them.
Aim for exuberance.
Take good care of your body.”
Scott Doorley loves to use ampersands: He is a designer, teacher, mentor, & father. He is the Creative Director of the Stanford d.school & designs spaces for people looking to bring creativity into their life & work. Occasionally, he finds time to be writer, speaker, & artist: Scott is co-author of the book of Make Space & his digital artwork has been exhibited at the San Jose Museum of Art, ZeroOne, & the Gray Area Foundation for the Arts.

Clark Scheffy

  • Managing Director at IDEO
  • website
  • twitter
“If you want to cultivate creativity, practice collaboration, taking risks, breaking the rules, and failing early and often.”
Clark Scheffy is a managing director at IDEO San Francisco. He focuses on the intersections of business strategy, brand, and designed experiences. In his current role lead a studio focused on cultivating new portfolios of business and evolving IDEO's offer. He has worked on projects for clients including JetBlue, Brooks Sports, Grainger, Converse, Steelcase, Snap-on, and Chase.
Prior to joining IDEO, Clark worked in the field of civil engineering for UC Berkeley. He was an editor for the …For Dummies series of best-selling how-to books, and has written several books for the brand. In the midst of all that, he toured as a professional musician and spent a couple of years as a chef.
Clark holds a BFA with high distinction in Industrial Design from the California College of the Arts, and a BA with honors in English Literature with a minor in Music Composition from the UC Berkeley.

Joshua Wolf Shenk

  • Author of Power of Two
  • website
  • twitter
  • facebook
“There is a vitality, a life force, a quickening that is translated through you into action… It is not your business to determine how good it is; nor how valuable it is; nor how it compares with other expressions. It is your business to keep it yours, clearly and directly, to keep the channel open...”
— Martha Graham to her friend Agnes de Mille
Joshua Wolf Shenk is a curator and author, most recently, of Powers of Two: Seeking the Essence of Innovation in Creative Pairs. His magazine pieces have appeared in Harper’s, Time, The Atlantic, Slate, The New Yorker, The New York Times, and other publications. His first book, Lincoln’s Melancholy, was named one of the best books of 2005 by The New York Times, The Washington Post, and The Atlanta-Journal Constitution. A member of The Moth’s general counsel, Josh also directs Arts in Mind, a conversation series on the creative arts and psychology. He has been engaged to talk and lead workshops on creativity by Microsoft, the International Society for the Performing Arts, the Alliance of Artist Communities, and Seattle's Project Room.

Kanyi Maqubela

  • website
  • twitter
“Two concepts are very misunderstood in our community: finding joy in your work, and failure as a tool for innovation. As I prefer to think of it, joy isn't pleasure; joy is purpose. And failure isn't meant to be celebrated, failure is meant only to be accepted. Success should be celebrated.”

Jeff Hull

  • Founder of Oaklandish
  • Founder of the Jejune Institute
  • website
“Experiences are the new things.”
Jeff Hull began his foray into the realm of public arts in 1979, when he was a child performer at Oakland's Children's Fairyland (a storybook theme park and unsung predecessor to Disneyland). Nascent time spent in this wonderland setting has inspired his life and work ever since. In 2000 he created the iconic Oaklandish campaign, a covert street-operation designed to raise awareness about the unique cultural legacy of the Town. Next, he launched the Jejune Institute, a ground breaking immersive narrative experiment, which is now the subject of an award-winning documentary film. He is the creative director of the Situational Design studio, Nonchalance, which is engrossed with the launch of their next mysterious offering.

Scott Witthoft

  • Faculty at Stanford d.school
  • Co-author of Make Space
  • website
  • twitter
“Constraints: understand and leverage the hell out of them. Better stated by musician, Bill Evans, on jazz: 'When I'm playing, I'm playing everything I play against the strict squareness of the original form.'”
Scott Witthoft is a product designer, design educator, and writer. He's interested in understanding & tweaking interactions among systems. This has included forensic structural engineering, space & furniture design, and curriculum design. He co-authored the book, Make Space, which is a tool for everyone interested in designing & creating environments to support creative collaboration. At the Stanford d.school, he teaches classes in human-centered design and storytelling & visual communication.

Lisa Rowland

  • Improv artist
  • website
  • facebook
“Make play your practice. Choose to notice things and be delighted by them. Nothing is more inspiring than everything around you.”
Lisa Rowland fell in love with improv at Stanford University under the coaching of Patricia Ryan Madson. She now travels the country and the world performing, teaching and training. A mainstage player at BATS Improv and a founding member of Awkward Dinner Party and Improv Playhouse of San Francisco, she also teaches improv in the theatre department at Stanford and coaches high school improv. Lisa delivers improv-based professional trainings in communication, collaboration, storytelling and design thinking. The most surreal part of her life is her yearly trip to Saudi Arabia, to teach improv to science and technology students.

Emi Kolawole

  • Editor-in-residence & Lecturer at Stanford d.school
  • website
  • twitter
“Find comfort in your creativity and seek discomfort in everything else.”
Emi is a multi-media professional with over a decade of experience. She has worked in television, web and print media. The founding editor of the Innovations section of The Washington Post, Emi was invited to serve as the editor-in-residence at the d.school at Stanford University, where she currently works on innovations in storytelling around the application of design thinking. Prior to that, Emi was a producer for The Washington Post's PostPolitics, the associate producer for "Washington Week with Gwen a Ifill" and a staff writer for FactCheck.org, covering the 2008 presidential election. Emi is a graduate of Wellesley College and American University. She is the Vice-curator of the Global Shapers Palo Alto hub -- an initiative of The World Economic Forum, and a French-American Foundation Young Leader.

John Bela

  • Urban Designer, Gehl Studio
  • Co-founder of Rebar and creator of Park(ing) Day
  • website
  • instagram
“Behind every crowd sourced, user-generated great idea is a solo visionary. How do you toggle between holding fiercely on to your personal, unique vision while accepting and absorbing the intelligence inherent in collaboration and working with the commons.”
John is an urbanist and public space designer based in San Francisco, Ca. He combines a background in art, science and environmental design to create vibrant, dynamic, and resilient urban human habitat. A pioneer in user-generated urbanism, John has successfully completed many projects that involve radical new formulations of social space. John recently joined Gehl Studio as an urban designer and landscape architect. Prior to joining Gehl, he was a cofounder and partner of Rebar Group, where he created some of the group’s most groundbreaking and memorable work such as: Park(ing) Day, Parklets, The Panhandle Bandshell, The Civic Center Victory Garden, Bubbleway, Parkcycle Swarm, and others.

Audrey Crane

  • Partner at DesignMap
  • website
  • twitter
“Think to yourself, 'Is it safe enough to try?'”
As a Partner at DesignMap, Audrey has the great pleasure to pitch in and help clients large and small, and at every stage of development, from The Bold Italic to ExactTarget to HP and lots of interesting challenges in between. Audrey has worked in high tech for 15 years.
